Part 1: De Casteljau's Algorithm for Bezier Curves

De Casteljau's algorithm is a recursive method for evaluating Bezier curves, a fundamental tool in vector graphics and animation for creating smooth, controllable curves. Implementing this algorithm involved interpolating between control points based on a parameter 't', which moves the curve from start to end. The process iteratively linearly interpolates between pairs of points, reducing the number of points at each step until a single point on the curve for a specific 't' value is determined.

For my custom Bezier curve, I chose 6 control points, creating a curve that showcases the algorithm's flexibility in crafting both simple and complex shapes. Through screenshots, I documented the step-by-step evolution of the curve, highlighting the algorithm's elegance in progressively refining the curve's path.

Part 2: Extending to Bezier Surfaces

Extending de Casteljau's algorithm to Bezier surfaces allowed me to create smooth, continuous surfaces. By treating the control points as a grid, I applied the algorithm first in one direction and then in the other, effectively evaluating Bezier curves along the grid's rows and columns. This two-step process resulted in a surface that can be finely controlled through its control points, as demonstrated by my implementation's ability to render a Bezier surface for the provided 'teapot.bez'.

Part 3: Area-Weighted Vertex Normals

Implementing area-weighted vertex normals involved calculating the normal for each vertex by averaging the normals of adjacent faces, weighted by their area. This technique significantly improves shading realism by smoothing out lighting across the surface. The comparison screenshots between flat and Phong shading on 'teapot.dae' distinctly show the smoother, more visually appealing result achieved with area-weighted normals.

Part 4: Edge Flip Operation

The edge flip operation is a fundamental mesh manipulation tool that adjusts connectivity to improve mesh quality or prepare for further subdivision. My implementation reassigns the connectivity between vertices, edges, and faces around the flipped edge, ensuring the mesh remains manifold. Debugging this part was particularly challenging, requiring careful tracking of half-edge connectivity. Part 6: Loop Subdivision

Loop subdivision is a method for refining triangle meshes to create smoother, more detailed surfaces. Implementing this required a multi-step approach: recalculating vertex positions based on neighboring vertices, splitting edges to introduce new vertices, and flipping certain edges to maintain mesh quality. Observations on subdivided meshes revealed the smoothing of sharp corners and edges, with a noted tendency toward asymmetry after multiple iterations. Pre-processing the mesh with strategic edge splits and flips proved effective in achieving more symmetric outcomes, as documented through the subdivision of 'cube.dae'.
